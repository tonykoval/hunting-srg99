Chapter 4 · Part II The SAT Verdict

The most direct attack is to write "$\Gamma$ is an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$" as a Boolean formula and let a solver decide it. A dedicated 2023 study did exactly that — and its central finding is a negative one that doubles as a structural insight: SAT solvers cannot finish, and the reason is that Conway's graph has too little symmetry to exploit.

4.1   The encoding, and why it explodes

Put one Boolean variable $x_{ij}$ on each of the $\binom{99}{2}=4851$ possible edges. The $\mu=2$ and $\lambda=1$ conditions are counting constraints on common neighbours, and each common-neighbour term $x_{it}x_{jt}$ is a product needing its own auxiliary variable ("cherry"). The bookkeeping is brutal:

Encoding size

Regularity alone, for one vertex, is $\binom{98}{13}+\binom{98}{15}\approx 1.8\times10^{17}$ clauses — so the degree constraints must be dropped (they are redundant, Ch 2). Even the $\lambda$/$\mu$ "cherry" constraints alone run to $\approx 7\times10^8$ clauses, about $22$ GB. Stabilising one vertex and its neighbourhood reduces the free part to $\tfrac{14}{2}\cdot\!\binom{}{}\!\ldots = 84$ unordered pairs, but the residual search space is still far beyond what a solver can traverse.

Crucially the blow-up is not solver-specific: CP-SAT and the competition-grade kissat fail identically. The bottleneck is the formula, not the back end.

4.2   The verdict

The empirical finding (Keramatipour 2023)

Encoding $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ as SAT — direct CNF, pseudo-Boolean, and a "triangular" $(231,18,5,\le3)$ reformulation — and running it yields, in every variant, no decision in reasonable time. The study traces this to the absence of exploitable symmetry: SAT succeeds on strongly regular graphs with large automorphism groups (where symmetry-breaking collapses the search) and fails on the asymmetric ones — and Conway's graph, if it exists, is asymmetric.

This is the same wall the sister volume Hunting SRG(37) documents from the other side: dynamic symmetry breaking is what makes a graph-search SAT instance tractable, and a graph with no symmetry offers nothing to break.

4.3   A sharper reformulation: minimise the claws

One idea from the study survives as a genuinely different lever. By $\lambda=1$, every neighbourhood must be a claw-free union of complete graphs — and for these parameters, exactly $7$ disjoint edges (Ch 2). Recast existence as an optimisation: hold $14$-regularity and $\mu=2$ as hard constraints, and minimise the number of claws ($K_{1,2}$) inside neighbourhoods as a soft objective.

Claw-minimisation (MAX-SAT)

If a $14$-regular, $\mu=2$ graph on $99$ vertices exists, then a graph minimising the number of neighbourhood claws attains zero claws — and a zero-claw graph has $\lambda=1$, hence is an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$. So the question becomes: can the claw count be driven to $0$? If the minimum is positive, no such graph exists.

This turns a satisfiability wall into an optimisation landscape — the same move the SRG(37) hunt makes with its defect-repair search. It does not beat the wall on a single machine, but it is the encoding most likely to reward a distributed or learning-guided attack.

4.4   The lesson

Wall: SAT needs symmetry, Conway's graph has none

Generic SAT/CP on $(99,14,1,2)$ is dominated by common-neighbour cardinality constraints ($10^8$+ clauses), and no back-end clears them. What would change the picture is symmetry to break — but every closed automorphism order (Chapter 3) says there is little or none. SAT is therefore the wrong tool for the asymmetric core, and the decisive work falls to geometry (Chapter 5) and distributed enumeration (Chapter 6).
