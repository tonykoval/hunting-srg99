Chapter 2 · Part I Spectrum & Local Structure

Two conditions — $\lambda=1$ and $\mu=2$ on $99$ vertices — already pin down the degree, the eigenvalues, and the exact shape of every neighbourhood. Conway's graph has no slack; that rigidity is both why it might not exist and why it is so hard to rule out.

2.1   The degree is forced

Theorem (14-regularity is automatic)

A graph on $99$ vertices in which every two adjacent vertices have exactly $\lambda=1$ common neighbour and every two non-adjacent vertices have exactly $\mu=2$ is necessarily $14$-regular.

Proof. Fix $v$ of degree $d$. The induced graph on $N(v)$ is a perfect matching (below), so $d$ is even. Each non-adjacent pair inside $N(v)$ needs a distinct common neighbour outside $N[v]$, giving $|V\setminus N[v]| = \binom{d}{2}-\tfrac{d}{2} = 99-d-1$, i.e. $\binom{d}{2}-\tfrac d2 = 98-d$. Solving, $d=14$. $\square$

So the "$14$" in $(99,14,1,2)$ is not an extra hypothesis — it is a consequence. The problem is genuinely just "$\lambda=1,\ \mu=2$, on $99$ points."

2.2   Every neighbourhood is seven disjoint edges

$\lambda=1$ means any two adjacent vertices share exactly one common neighbour. Inside $N(v)$ (the $14$ neighbours of $v$), each neighbour $u$ is adjacent to $v$, so $u$ and $v$ share exactly one common neighbour — which must lie in $N(v)$. Thus every vertex of $N(v)$ has degree exactly $1$ within $N(v)$: the neighbourhood induces a perfect matching, $7$ disjoint edges.

No claws allowed

Equivalently, $N(v)$ contains no path $P_3$ (no "claw" $K_{1,2}$): if it did, the centre would have two neighbours in $N(v)$, forcing a second common neighbour with $v$ and breaking $\lambda=1$. So $N(v)$ is a disjoint union of complete graphs, and $\lambda=1$ makes each a single edge — a $7$-edge matching. This is the local fingerprint every search in Part II exploits.

2.3   The spectrum

The non-principal eigenvalues solve $x^2-(\lambda-\mu)x-(k-\mu) = x^2 + x - 12 = (x-3)(x+4) = 0$, so $r=3$, $s=-4$. Multiplicities come from $1+f+g=99$ and $14+3f-4g=\mathrm{tr}(A)=0$:

Spectrum of any SRG(99,14,1,2)

$\mathrm{spec}(A) = \{\,14^{1},\ \ 3^{54},\ \ (-4)^{44}\,\}.$

Integral multiplicities ($f=54$, $g=44$). The large multiplicity $44$ of $s=-4$ is the quiet villain of the story: it is exactly what makes the triangle-based geometric filters fail to engage (Chapter 5).

2.4   The triangle world

With $99\cdot14/2 = 693$ edges, each in a unique triangle, the graph carries exactly $693/3 = \mathbf{231}$ triangles. These triangles are not incidental — they encode the whole graph, and the existence of $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ is equivalent to the existence of a related combinatorial structure on those $231$ triangles (a strongly-regular-graph parameter set in its own right, $(231,18,\,\cdot\,,\le 3)$). Several attacks in Part II move between these two views.
