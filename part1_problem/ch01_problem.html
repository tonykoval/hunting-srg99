Chapter 1 · Part I Conway's $1000 Problem

In 2014 John Conway placed a $1000 bounty on a single graph: does there exist a strongly regular graph $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$? Equivalently — a graph on $99$ vertices in which every edge lies in exactly one triangle and every non-edge in exactly one quadrilateral. A decade later, nobody has collected.

1.1   The question

Conway's "99-graph problem" is one of his five $1000 problems. Stated as a strongly regular graph, the requirement is:

The Conway 99-graph

A graph on $99$ vertices, $14$-regular, in which any two adjacent vertices have exactly $\lambda=1$ common neighbour (so every edge sits in a unique triangle) and any two non-adjacent vertices have exactly $\mu=2$ common neighbours (so every non-edge sits in a unique quadrilateral). Does one exist? Open.

The degree $14$ is not a free choice — it is forced by the local rules (Chapter 2). So the whole problem is the bare pair of conditions "$\lambda=1$, $\mu=2$ on $99$ vertices", which is what makes it so clean, and so stubborn.

1.2   Where the problem comes from

The graph is named for Conway, but the question is older than the bounty. In 1969, Norman Biggs raised the existence of an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$; J. J. Seidel, the architect of much of strongly-regular-graph theory, took it up and kept it alive for decades. The parameters sit in the orbit of Higman's work on the third Conway sporadic simple group $\mathrm{Co}_3$ and the Higman–Sims graph — the dense, highly symmetric world from which so many strongly regular graphs descend, and against which Conway's sparse, near-rigid graph is a pointed exception.

In 2017 John Conway placed it on his short list of five problems he would pay $\$1000$ each to see solved — alongside the thrackle conjecture and the dead-fly problem. It is the only one of the five about a single, concrete graph: either you exhibit it or you prove it cannot be. A decade later the cheque is uncashed, which is precisely why a hunt log like this one is worth keeping.

A problem with a price tag

Few open problems in combinatorics come with a number attached. The $\$1000$ is a small fraction of the compute the search has already consumed — but it is a useful reminder that this is not a routine classification gap. It is a named, advertised, decade-old challenge.

1.3   Why it resists

$(99,14,1,2)$ passes every feasibility test — integral spectrum, Krein conditions, the absolute bound, the $\mu$-bound — so non-existence is not visible at the parameter level. Yet no construction is known, and the most powerful general tools bounce off it for a reason that recurs throughout this book:

  • It is sparse ($k/n = 14/99 \approx 0.14$), so the algebraic machinery that thrives on dense graphs has little to grip.
  • If it exists, its symmetry is small — most automorphism orders have already been ruled out — so symmetry-reduction has little to remove.
  • And $\lambda=1$ makes the local structure rigid but the global spectrum ($s=-4$ with multiplicity $44$) too large for the triangle-based geometric filters to engage (Chapter 5).

1.4   The shape of the hunt

The search proceeds on three fronts, each a chapter of Part II. Reduce by symmetry: assume an automorphism, quotient, and rule the order out if no graph appears — this has closed orders $11$ and $3$ and isolated the famous order-$7$ door. Encode as logic: hand "$\Gamma$ is an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$" to a SAT solver — which, as a dedicated 2023 study showed, cannot finish, and the reason why is itself a result. Constrain by geometry: embed the would-be graph in Euclidean space and propagate the spectral conditions.

The two endings

Existence. One valid adjacency matrix — from a lifted orbit matrix or a completed search — and Conway's graph exists, a $\$1000$ answer to a famous question.
Non-existence. Every automorphism order closed and the rigid space exhausted, and $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ is refuted.

This book is the honest map of how far each front has reached — what is proven, what the famous order-$7$ case actually costs, and why the problem remains, for now, Conway's.
