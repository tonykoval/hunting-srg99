Chapter 6 · Part III Findings & the Frontier

Conway's prize is unclaimed, but the hunt has mapped the terrain precisely: what is closed, the three walls and their single common cause, and the few doors still ajar. Here is the state of play and exactly what either ending requires.

6.2   Three walls, one cause

The common cause: Conway's graph is (almost) asymmetric

Symmetry closes order by order but leaves the lone order-$7$ action and the involutions — because there is little symmetry to find. SAT drowns in cardinality clauses — because there is no symmetry to break. Geometry fails to over-constrain the triangles — because the rank-$44$ embedding is too roomy. All three are the same fact: if Conway's graph exists, it is sparse and nearly rigid, exactly the regime in which every general method pays full enumeration cost.

6.3   The doors still ajar

  • Order $7$: the configuration $[1,7^{14}]$. The unique Cesarz–Woldar action; $281$ M solver nodes in an hour did not exhaust it. Cluster-scale, and the single most famous open door.
  • Involutions ($p=2$). $46$ feasible configurations, each an orbit-matrix run — the largest unfinished symmetric block.
  • Cyclic orders $9,33,99$ and small non-abelian groups. Still to be swept.
  • Fully rigid graphs. The asymmetric core, reached only by the claw-minimisation optimisation (Ch 4) or the Gram-PSD enumeration (Ch 5).

6.4   The decision procedure

Two endings

Existence. One lifted orbit matrix — from the order-$7$ door, an involution, or any other case — or one completed adjacency matrix from the claw-minimisation / Gram search, is Conway's graph: the $\$1000$ answer.
Non-existence. Every automorphism order closed UNSAT and the rigid space exhausted with zero claws unreachable, and $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ is refuted — joining $(85,14,3,2)$ in the column of settled-by-non-existence.

A timeout settles nothing; both endings need real certificates.

6.5   The plan

  1. Drive the order-$7$ door to a verdict. Run the $[1,7^{14}]$ orbit-matrix enumeration to completion on a cluster — a lift wins the prize, an UNSAT strengthens Cesarz–Woldar.
  2. Finish the involution sweep. Distribute the $46$ configurations; clearing them closes "no even-order symmetry".
  3. Push the claw-minimisation / Gram search. The asymmetric core, on the optimisation encoding most amenable to distributed and learning-guided attack.

Conway's $99$-graph is the most famous open door in the strongly-regular tables. A decade on, it is still closed — but the map of how to push it is, at last, complete. This book is that map.
