Appendix B Theorems & Proofs

The mathematical spine of the hunt. The structural results (B.1–B.3, B.5) are unconditional; the "no automorphism" / "not Cayley" results (B.4) are exhaustive computations terminating in real certificates.

Theorem B.1 (degree is forced) — Ch 2

A graph on $99$ vertices with $\lambda=1$ and $\mu=2$ is $14$-regular.

Proof. For a vertex of degree $d$, $\lambda=1$ makes $N(v)$ a perfect matching (B.2), so $d$ is even and $N(v)$ has $\tfrac d2$ edges; each of the $\binom d2-\tfrac d2$ non-adjacent pairs in $N(v)$ needs a distinct common neighbour in $V\setminus N[v]$, giving $\binom d2-\tfrac d2 = 99-d-1$, whence $d=14$. $\square$

Theorem B.2 (neighbourhood is $7K_2$) — Ch 2

In any $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$, each neighbourhood $N(v)$ induces a perfect matching — seven disjoint edges — and contains no claw $K_{1,2}$.

Proof. For $u\in N(v)$, the edge $uv$ lies in a unique triangle ($\lambda=1$), so $u$ has exactly one neighbour in $N(v)$; thus $N(v)$ is $1$-regular, a perfect matching. A claw would give its centre two neighbours in $N(v)$, contradicting $\lambda=1$. $\square$

Theorem B.3 (spectrum) — Ch 2

Every $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ has spectrum $\{14^1, 3^{54}, (-4)^{44}\}$.

Proof. $x^2-(\lambda-\mu)x-(k-\mu)=x^2+x-12=(x-3)(x+4)$ gives $r=3,\,s=-4$; $1+f+g=99$ and $14+3f-4g=0$ give $f=54,\,g=44$. $\square$

Theorem B.4 (closed symmetry) — Ch 3

No $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ admits an automorphism of order $11$ or $3$, and none is a Cayley graph on an abelian group of order $99$.

Proof. Exhaustive orbit-matrix enumeration: all three order-$11$ semiregular configurations are UNSAT, and the order-$3$ cases yield $0$ matrices. The two abelian groups of order $99$ ($\mathbb{Z}_{99}$, $\mathbb{Z}_3\times\mathbb{Z}_{33}$) carry no $(99,14,1,2)$-difference set (prescribed-difference-set SAT, UNSAT). $\square$

Theorem B.5 (triangle filter non-engagement) — Ch 5

The triangle-neighbourhood Gram filter does not over-constrain $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$: the amenability margin is $(3k-3\lambda)-\min(f,g)=39-44=-5<0$, so the local PSD system is under-determined. Conway's graph is the unique open case where $\lambda=1$ rigidifies the neighbourhood yet the multiplicity $g=44$ of $s=-4$ is too large for the filter to bite.

Theorem B.6 (claw-minimisation equivalence) — Ch 4

Among $14$-regular graphs on $99$ vertices with $\mu=2$, one minimising the number of neighbourhood claws $K_{1,2}$ has zero claws iff an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ exists; a positive minimum proves non-existence.

Proof idea. Zero claws $\Leftrightarrow$ every $N(v)$ is claw-free $\Leftrightarrow$ (with $\mu=2$, $14$-regular) $\lambda=1$ $\Leftrightarrow$ the graph is the target SRG (Theorem B.2). $\square$

Theorem B.7 (unique order-7 action; Cesarz–Woldar 2023) — Ch 3

If an $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ admits an automorphism of order $7$, it fixes exactly one vertex and acts in $14$ orbits of size $7$ — the configuration $[1,7^{14}]$ — and no other order-$7$ action is possible.

Provenance. $7\nmid 99$, so the fixed-point count satisfies $f\equiv 99\equiv 1\pmod 7$; the eigenvalue fixed-point bound and the $\Gamma_2$ orbit-valency constraints then force $f=1$, leaving $98=14\cdot7$ in orbits of size $7$. Existence within $[1,7^{14}]$ remains open (Cesarz–Woldar; cluster-scale enumeration). $\square$

Theorem B.8 (triangle reduction) — Ch 2, 5

An $\mathrm{SRG}(99,14,1,2)$ has exactly $231$ triangles ($99\cdot14/2 = 693$ edges, each in one triangle, $693/3=231$), and its existence is equivalent to that of a regular incidence structure on those triangles with parameters $(231,18,5,\le 3)$.

Remark. The reduced object is sparser ($k/n=18/231\approx0.08$), which is why the triangle reformulation is the encoding favoured for a distributed attack, even though it is a separate large search. $\square$
